Hello Humans!

Have you felt that in life, every time you try something, there are blocks? Turns out, the science of human data acknowledges that experience, and can find solutions to get around those blocks, if it is done correctly!

My data room is equipped with tools to properly analyse human data samples to determine their possible generalizability to actual collection of humans we are observing. There are a lot of checkpoints here, and it’s all based in the practice of statistics on human data.

Today, these practices fall into the field of artificial intelligence (AI) using data mining tools (extracting human data samples) such as machine learning (ML) which looks at commonalities amongst individuals (clustering) or their characteristics (principal component analysis (PCA)). My goal is to find valid human data samples for statistical learning, so I can put mathematical patterns to the data. This is based on a well-established field of statistics.

Humans, “Great. So how is it going to help me? Where’s the utility?”

My goal in presenting data is similar to that of a medical doctor: to be a teacher. It has been argued that medical doctors are the best teachers in the world. They take incredibly complicated medical data from a patient, and are able to explain it to them in a few minutes. They explain what the conditions are, what the concern is, and what to do about it, in a way that when the patient walks out of their appointment, they have a good solid overall understanding. That’s amazing!

Similarly, my goal is the same as the medical doctor: To be a teacher of data, taking the results of very complicated statistical and computer computations, and explaining the meaning of it to end users, in a few minutes.

STATISTICS - THE SCIENCE OF DATA
Statistics is the science of collecting, organizing, and interpreting numerical facts, which we call data. Data are numbers with context. In practice, any set of data is accompanied by background information that helps us understand the data. When starting new project, the following are the first questions we ask:
  • why important: why?
    • What purpose does the data have?
    • Do we hope to answer some specific questions?
    • Do we want to draw conclusions about individuals other than those for whom we actually have the data?
  • what's it about? who?
    • What individuals do the data describe?
    • How many individuals appear in the data?
  • details? what?
    • How many variables do the data contain?
    • What are the exact definitions of these variables? In what unit of measure is each variable recorded?
